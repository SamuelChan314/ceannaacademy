Plot and structure

Summary

Overall Summary

Death of a Salesman follows the final twenty-four hours of Willy Loman, an aging traveling salesman whose professional identity, family relationships, and inner sense of value collapse at the same time. The play is not strictly realistic or chronological: Willy’s memories, fantasies, regrets, and imagined conversations interrupt the present until the audience experiences his mind from the inside.

Willy’s central conflict is not only economic failure. It is the damage caused when human identity depends on external success. He cannot accept ordinary limitation because he equates ordinariness with worthlessness. Biff’s truth threatens Willy, Linda’s loyalty protects him, Happy repeats him, and the Requiem leaves the audience holding Willy’s foolishness, victimhood, love, and delusion together.

How to Read the Play

  • The past is not background; it invades the present.
  • Willy’s dream is personal, but Miller also makes it social and economic.
  • Biff’s truth-telling is painful, not magically successful.
  • The ending asks whether Willy is foolish, responsible, victimized, loving, or all of these at once.
